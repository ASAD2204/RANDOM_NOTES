Introduction to Artificial Intelligence

What AI is, how it differs from ML/DL, and why it matters in real systems.

▶ What is AI?

Artificial Intelligence is the discipline of building computational systems that can perceive, reason, learn, and act toward goals. In software products, AI is usually implemented as a pipeline: input data is transformed into features/tokens, a model infers outputs, and business logic translates outputs into actions.

▶ Types of AI

  • Narrow AI: specialized for specific domains (today’s production reality).
  • General AI: broad human-level adaptability across domains (research goal).
  • Super AI: intelligence beyond human capability (theoretical concept).

▶ AI vs ML vs DL

AI (broad umbrella)
└─ ML (models learn from data)
     └─ DL (deep neural architectures)

Not all AI is ML (rule-based systems are AI), and not all ML is deep learning (trees, linear models, SVMs are ML but not DL).

▶ Real-world applications

  • Personalized recommendations
  • Fraud/risk detection
  • Medical image triage and diagnostics support
  • Autonomous assistants and copilots
  • Enterprise search and knowledge automation

▶ Theory Deep Dive

AI engineering is fundamentally decision-making under uncertainty. Every model output has confidence and error bounds. The practical question is not only “Can the model predict?” but “Can the system make safe, cost-effective decisions at scale?” This introduces key design concerns: calibration, fallback behavior, monitoring, and human oversight where needed.

  • Representation: map raw reality into machine-readable form.
  • Inference: estimate likely outcomes from learned patterns.
  • Control: apply policies, thresholds, and guardrails before action.
  • Feedback: measure production outcomes to improve future behavior.

🌟 Key subfields

Machine learning, deep learning, NLP, computer vision, robotics, planning, reinforcement learning, and knowledge representation.
